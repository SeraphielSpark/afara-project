Ubuntu — I Am Because We Are

Florence Cook

Elder & Cultural Director

1,204 342 15 Feb 2026

In the Nguni languages of Southern Africa, there is a word that defies simple translation into English. That word is Ubuntu. Often reduced to "humanity" or "I am because we are," it is, in truth, an entire cosmological and ethical framework—a way of moving through the world that insists our individual humanity is inextricably bound to the humanity of others.

To understand Ubuntu is to recognize that a person is a person through other people. We do not enter the world fully formed; we are shaped by the hands that hold us, the communities that raise us, and the ancestors who paved the way. When one of us suffers, the collective suffers. When one of us thrives, the collective is elevated.

"My humanity is caught up, is inextricably bound up, in yours. We belong in a bundle of life."

This philosophy did not remain static on the continent. When millions of our ancestors were violently displaced during the transatlantic slave trade, they carried the essence of Ubuntu across the ocean. It was this deeply ingrained understanding of collective survival that allowed enslaved communities to forge new kinship networks on plantations, replacing biological families torn apart by the auction block.

It is the same spirit you witness in the mutual aid societies of post-emancipation African American communities, in the "pardner" or "susu" saving systems of the Caribbean, and in the joyous, collective rhythms of a Brazilian samba circle. The oppressor sought to individualize and isolate; the African spirit insisted on the collective.

Today, the Diaspora finds itself navigating a deeply individualized, hyper-capitalist global society. We are often taught to measure our worth by our personal accumulation of wealth or status. Ubuntu calls us back to a different standard. It asks us to look at our neighbor, our brother in Brixton, our sister in Bahia, and recognize our shared reflection.

As we build Wisdom of Afara, this is the cornerstone. We cannot heal alone. We cannot recover our history alone. The work of the Diaspora is not merely personal discovery; it is collective remembrance. We are, because the family is.
